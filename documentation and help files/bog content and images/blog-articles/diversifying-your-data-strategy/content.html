Diversifying Your Data Strategy

  • Baylen Springer
  • / May 8, 2023

In today’s data-driven world, it’s more important than ever to incorporate a variety of data sources into your data strategy. Incorporating external data sources into your analytics and data science practices is essential for making informed business decisions. By analyzing data from different sources, you can gain valuable insights into market trends, customer behavior, and much more.

How do I use Economic Data in my Data Strategy?

How Economic Data Can Drive Cost Reductions and Improve Forecasting

Incorporating economic data sources into your analytics and data science practices can lead to cost reductions and better forecasting for businesses. By leveraging current data sources, businesses can gain insights into economic trends and patterns that can help them optimize their operations, minimize costs, and make better-informed decisions. There are several studies that highlight the importance of incorporating economic data sources into marketing and analytics.

Research on the Importance of Economic Data

Here are a few examples:

  • A study by McKinsey & Company found that companies that incorporate macroeconomic data into their forecasting and planning processes can improve their sales forecasts by up to 20%.
  • A report by Deloitte found that incorporating external data, including economic data, into marketing analytics can improve marketing performance by up to 60%.
  • According to a survey by the DMA, 56% of marketers use economic data to inform their marketing strategies.
  • A study by Forrester found that companies that use economic data to inform their marketing strategies can increase their marketing ROI by up to 40%.

These statistics suggest that incorporating economic data sources into marketing and analytics can lead to significant benefits for businesses, including improved sales forecasting, better marketing performance, and higher marketing ROI.

To leverage these benefits, businesses can turn to a variety of online sources that provide access to a wealth of economic data and insights.

Sources of Economic Data

The U.S. Department of Labor provides information on employment, wages, and labor productivity. This data can help businesses make informed decisions about workforce planning, compensation strategies, and talent acquisition. The U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis provides information on GDP, consumer spending, and corporate profits, which can help businesses understand the overall economic climate and identify opportunities for growth.

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics also provides data on inflation, productivity, and labor market trends. This information can help businesses optimize their supply chain, pricing strategies, and operations. The U.S. Census Bureau provides demographic data, including age, gender, income, and education level, which can help businesses target their marketing efforts more effectively.

The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis and the United States Federal Reserve provide economic data on interest rates, money supply, and credit availability. This data can help businesses make informed decisions about financing and investment strategies. The Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System provides data on the U.S. economy, monetary policy, and financial stability, which can help businesses navigate market volatility and uncertainty.

The U.S. Energy Information Administration provides information on energy production, consumption, and prices. This data can help businesses optimize their energy usage and costs. The Federal Housing Finance Agency provides data on housing prices, which can help businesses make informed decisions about real estate investment and development.

Other valuable economic data sources include the U.S. Department of Treasury, Freddie Mac, NOAA, the National Association of Realtors, the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development, S&P Dow Jones, the COVID Tracking Project, the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office, and the Center for Disease Control and Prevention. Each of these data sources provides unique insights that can help businesses make better-informed decisions.

Demographic data provides insights into the characteristics of a particular population, such as age, gender, education level, and income, which can help businesses tailor their products and services, optimize their marketing efforts, and make better-informed decisions.

Research on the Importance of Demographic Data

Here are a few examples:

One of the key sources of demographic data is the U.S. Census Bureau, which provides detailed information on population size, composition, and distribution. This data can help businesses identify target markets and optimize their marketing strategies. For example, if a business is targeting young adults, they can use census data to identify areas with a high concentration of young adults and tailor their marketing efforts accordingly.

By incorporating Demographic data into your marketing strategy, you can make more informed decisions that will drive sales and increase your revenue.

This data can help businesses identify labor market trends and optimize their workforce planning and talent acquisition strategies.

Another important demographic data source is the National Association of Realtors, which provides data on home ownership, rental rates, and housing prices. This data can help businesses make informed decisions about real estate investment and development.
